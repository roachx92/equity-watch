## Executive summary — investment thesis

**What this company is.** Coherent Corp. is a ~$7B-revenue (FY2026 run-rate) vertically-integrated photonics and compound-semiconductor manufacturer. It grows its own indium phosphide (InP) and gallium arsenide wafers, fabricates its own lasers — EMLs, continuous-wave DFBs, VCSELs — and assembles them into optical transceivers (800G/1.6T datacom, 400ZR/800ZR coherent), external laser source modules, and optical circuit switches. It also runs a materially smaller Industrial segment (lasers, materials) that is now shrinking and being pruned by divestiture. As of the reorganization effective 2025-07-01 there are two reportable segments: **Datacenter & Communications (D&C)** and **Industrial**. D&C was 75% of Q3 FY26 revenue and is the entire equity story. [P]

**What the debate actually is.** It is **not** a quality debate and it is **not** a solvency debate — it is a **durability-and-price** debate, and the two have been conflated by the tape. The operating business is demonstrably strong and improving. The stock has fallen 36.9% from its 3 June high. Those two statements are both true, and reconciling them is the whole exercise.

**The three hard anchor facts.**

1. **NVIDIA bought 7,788,161 shares at $256.80 on 2026-03-02 for $2.0B in cash** — a negotiated private placement under §4(a)(2), paired with a non-exclusive multi-billion-dollar purchase commitment, capacity and access rights across **five additional product families** related to co-packaged optics, and a capacity agreement running **through 2030**. [P — 8-K acc. 0001193125-26-084366; 10-Q Note 15] *The current price of $277.60 is only **8.1% above** what the single best-informed strategic buyer in the industry paid four months ago after full diligence.*
2. **Q3 FY26 D&C revenue was $1,361.7M, +41% YoY, at a 25.5% segment margin** — the second consecutive quarter of double-digit sequential growth, with record bookings and backlog extending into calendar 2028. Leverage fell from 2.1x a year ago to **0.5x**; the entire $2.5B Series B preferred was converted and retired in the December 2025 quarter. [P — 10-Q; Q3 call]
3. **9M FY26 operating cash flow was $10.1M, against $503.3M in the prior-year period, on net earnings that *rose* to $551M.** Inventories consumed $699M of cash (WIP alone went $824M → $1,238M) and capex nearly doubled to $547M. Free cash flow for the nine months is roughly **−$537M**. [P — 10-Q cash-flow statement] *This is the single most important number in the filings and it is not in any headline.*

**Three reasons to own — each with its honest counterweight.**

**Reason 1 — The moat sits in the laser die, and it is architecture-agnostic.** The genuinely defensible asset is InP epitaxy and high-power CW laser design plus owned 6-inch InP fab capacity in Sherman, Texas, which management says yields **">4x as many devices at less than half the cost"** versus 3-inch. Global InP epitaxy is concentrated in a handful of houses in Taiwan, Japan and Germany; most module vendors are hostage to them, and Coherent is not. Critically, **every competing interconnect architecture consumes InP lasers** — a 1.6T EML pluggable, a SiPho pluggable, and a co-packaged switch with an external laser source all need InP light. Coherent is therefore not making an architecture bet; it is selling picks to every side of the argument. *Counterweight:* the moat is in the **die**, not the **module**. Module assembly has no durable moat, and the evidence is unambiguous — Eoptolink went from #7 to #3 in LightCounting's ranking in a single year [T], and Chinese assemblers earn better net margins than Coherent does. If the external-laser-source module standardizes under OCP the way pluggables did, Coherent ends up owning the die and renting the module: high volume, compressing margin. That is the same trap the transceiver business already fell into once.

**Reason 2 — The balance sheet was rebuilt, decisively, in twelve months.** Total debt fell $493M in nine months to $3,194M *while simultaneously drawing $1.25B of incremental Term A*; Term B is down 61% from its original $2,800M to $1,080M, of which **$502M of the $509M paid on the term facilities was voluntary**. Against $1,593M cash plus $825M short-term investments, net debt is roughly **$776M**. There is no maturity before 7/1/2029, no convertible notes outstanding, no ATM program, and the revolver is undrawn with $668M available. S&P upgraded the credit to 'BB' in April 2026. [P — 10-Q Note 8; T for the rating] *Counterweight:* that strength is partly *purchased*, not earned — $2.0B came from NVIDIA and $437M from divesting the aerospace & defense business and a Munich product division. Strip those and the nine-month cash picture is a $537M free-cash-flow deficit. The balance sheet is presently carried by a strategic investor and asset sales, not by operations, and that will remain true until the $2.1B inventory converts.

**Reason 3 — The stock has de-rated ~37% while the operating evidence improved.** Nothing in the primary filings deteriorated between the 3 June high and today. There has been no guidance cut, no lost contract, no covenant issue, no customer loss, no negative pre-announcement, and no company-specific news at all on the two largest down days. Short interest is only ~3.34% of shares outstanding [T], which rules out a short-driven mechanic — this is **long liquidation**, not a squeeze unwind. Consensus sits at roughly $380–384 against a $277.60 price. [T] *Counterweight:* a de-rate is not automatically an opportunity. The stock ran from ~$184.57 to $426.89 in five months on a narrative — co-packaged optics as an imminent, enormous, incremental market — and part of that narrative genuinely got pushed to the right on 9 June. **A multiple built on a 2027 story is correctly lower when the story becomes a 2028 story, even if not one dollar of current earnings changes.** The fundamentals did not break; the *timeline* did, and the timeline was in the multiple.

**What has to be true.** All four of: (a) hyperscaler AI capex holds near its guided ~$725B 2026 aggregate and does not digest hard in 2027; (b) the $2.1B inventory and the 9M $547M capex convert into shipped revenue on roughly the guided schedule rather than sitting idle; (c) Coherent defends its ~10–15% [T3] share of high-speed datacom against Innolight and Eoptolink, who are currently growing two to three times faster; and (d) the external-laser-source socket does **not** commoditize into a four-source race that strips module margin. Fail (b) and it is an earnings problem within four quarters. Fail (d) and it is a terminal-multiple problem.

**Bottom line.** The operating fundamentals have **not** deteriorated — that claim survives contact with the filings, and this report documents it line by line in the Special Section below. But the honest version of the sentence is narrower than the comforting one: *earnings power is intact and improving; the multiple was inflated by a co-packaged-optics adoption curve that has since been pushed out roughly a year; and cash conversion has genuinely deteriorated in a way that is defensible as a ramp but is not yet proven.* Two of those three are unchanged. The third is real, it is new, and it is in the cash-flow statement rather than the press release. **The 13 August FY2026 Q4 print is where all three resolve.**

### C. The analytical crux — is a CPO delay even bearish for Coherent?

This is where the market's reaction and the underlying economics genuinely diverge, and it is the most important paragraph in this report.

**The market sold Coherent on news that a competing architecture is arriving later than expected.** Consider what that actually means for each of Coherent's two exposures:

- **Pluggable transceivers (the large, current, revenue-generating business).** A CPO delay is **unambiguously good**. Every quarter CPO does not ramp is a quarter in which 800G and 1.6T pluggable modules — Coherent's biggest revenue line, with 1.6T guided to be *accretive* to transceiver gross margin — remain the only way to move the bits. The SemiAnalysis thesis, taken at face value, **extends the life of Coherent's core franchise by roughly a year.**
- **CPO content (the future, contracted, not-yet-revenue business).** A CPO delay defers this. But note what Coherent actually sells into CPO: it is not the switch and not the optical engine — it is the **external laser source module** and its contents. Per CEO Jim Anderson on the Q3 call: *"In a CPO solution, we are not just providing the high-power CW laser; we provide the external laser source module, the fiber attach unit — including microlens arrays and polarization-maintaining fiber"* — plus isolators and thermoelectric coolers inside the ELS. [P] Both NVIDIA and Broadcom deliberately keep high-power lasers **off** the switch package, in faceplate ELS modules, because lasers degrade next to a hot ASIC. **A CPO switch still needs Coherent's lasers.**

**So Coherent is on both sides of the trade.** The architecture shift moves *where* the laser sits, not *whether* one is needed. And decisively: **both EML-based and silicon-photonics-based 1.6T transceivers, and CPO external laser sources, all consume indium phosphide CW lasers from the same fab.** The 6-inch InP expansion in Sherman serves every architecture. This is why the capacity ramp is the correct thing for management to be spending on regardless of which way the CPO argument resolves, and it is why the moat is real in a way the module business is not.

**The honest counterweight — and it is substantial.** Three things keep this from being a clean "the market is simply wrong" story:

1. **The narrative premium was real and it deserved to deflate.** The stock went from ~$184.57 on 1 January to $426.89 on 2 June — a 131% move in five months — substantially on the CPO story, which management sized at *">$15 billion of incremental addressable market."* That figure is **company-stated and promotional**: Yole independently estimates the CPO market at **$8.1B by 2030** and LightCounting at **~$10B by 2030** [T], i.e. Coherent's number is roughly 1.5–2x credible third-party estimates and its scope is undefined. When a multiple is built on a company-stated TAM arriving in 2027 and the arrival moves to 2028–29, a lower multiple is the *correct* answer, not an irrational one.
2. **A delay is not free.** Coherent has committed to "incremental investments in equipment, labor, and working capital... through 2030" under the NVIDIA capacity agreement [P]. It has already spent $547M of capex in nine months and built $699M of inventory. **Pre-built capacity meeting a delayed demand curve is precisely how a ramp becomes an impairment.** The delay risk is real; it just expresses itself in the balance sheet rather than in the CPO narrative.
3. **The direction of share is genuinely unfavourable.** Coherent's datacenter revenue grew 37% YoY. Innolight grew 105% YoY in Q4'25 and Eoptolink cleared $1B a quarter [T]. **Growing at a third to a half the rate of your two closest competitors is losing share, even while growing strongly.** Eoptolink moved #7 → #3 in one year. This is the one fundamental that has been quietly deteriorating for three years, and it has nothing to do with CPO or the sell-off.

### D. Verdict — did the fundamentals change?

**On the evidence: no on earnings power, no on the balance sheet, yes on one thing — and it is not the thing the market sold.**

- **Earnings power: unchanged.** No guidance revision, no lost contract, no customer disclosure, no negative pre-announcement. The last reported quarter grew 21% with the lead segment at +41%. Backlog extends into 2028 and no cancellation has been disclosed.
- **Balance sheet: unchanged and materially stronger than a year ago.** 0.5x leverage from 2.1x, $2.4B of cash and short-term investments, no maturity before FY2029, no converts, no ATM, S&P upgrade in April.
- **Competitive position: unchanged by the sell-off, but structurally drifting.** The share-loss trend versus Innolight and Eoptolink predates all of this and continues. That is the genuine fundamental concern, and the market is not currently trading on it.
- **What genuinely changed: the CPO adoption timeline, and therefore the terminal multiple — not the earnings.** A one-year pushout in a >$8B-by-2030 incremental market is a real change to the discounted value of the optionality. It is not a change to FY2027 EPS.
- **What is genuinely new and under-discussed: cash conversion.** OCF of $10.1M on $551M of net earnings is the one metric that meaningfully deteriorated in FY2026, and it was disclosed on 6 May — *before* the June peak. The market bought the stock to $440 with that number already public and is now selling it for unrelated reasons. Both the buying and the selling look poorly informed by the actual filings.

**The plain-English answer to "have the fundamentals changed":** No — the business is doing what it said it would do, and is better capitalized than at any point in a decade. But do not take that as blanket reassurance. The stock is still up 50% year-to-date and 229% off its 52-week low; a 37% drawdown from a vertical run is a *multiple* event, not a *franchise* event, and the multiple that is compressing was inflated by a timeline that has now moved. The thing that would genuinely break this thesis is not CPO — it is $2.1B of inventory failing to convert. Watch the cash-flow statement on 13 August, not the headline.

## 1. Business model & role in the value chain

Coherent makes money by converting compound-semiconductor materials science into shipped photonic hardware. It operates the full stack: it grows InP and GaAs crystal, runs epitaxy (MOCVD/MBE), fabricates laser die (EMLs, CW DFBs, VCSELs, photodiodes), and packages them into modules and systems. Effective 2025-07-01 the reporting structure was reorganized from three segments (Networking / Materials / Lasers) into two, with prior periods recast and goodwill reallocated by relative fair value ($1,038M + $241M + $3,191M → **D&C $1,151M / Industrial $3,321M**); an impairment test run immediately before and after found no impairment. *Note: segment profit excludes stock-based compensation, amortization, restructuring and unallocated corporate costs ($82.1M in Q3) — it is **not** operating income and must not be treated as such.* [P]

**Position in the value chain — what Coherent is NOT.** It is not a switch-silicon vendor (that is Broadcom, NVIDIA, Marvell, Cisco). It is not a DSP vendor (Marvell, Broadcom, Credo, Semtech). It is not a foundry (TSMC, GlobalFoundries), not an OSAT (ASE, Amkor), and not a contract manufacturer (Fabrinet, Foxconn, Celestica). It sits one layer below all of them, as the **merchant supplier of the light itself** and of the modules that carry it. Its distinguishing feature versus most module competitors is that it owns the wafer and the die rather than buying them.

**Which segment is the equity story and which is ballast.** D&C is 75% of revenue, is growing 41%, carries the higher margin, and is the entire investment case. Industrial is shrinking 16% YoY, is being actively pruned (aerospace & defense sold 2025-09-02 for ~$400M at a $115M gain; a Munich product division sold 2026-01-30 at a $9M gain; two further entities held for sale with $20.1M of additional impairment), and functions as ballast plus a source of divestiture cash. [P] Investors are buying a datacenter optics company that still carries an industrial-laser business on the side.

### Moat verdict — **MODERATE and EMERGING**, wide in one layer and near-absent in another

The single most important analytical move here is to **refuse to average the two layers together.**

- **Proprietary and defensible (wide, durable): InP epitaxy, high-power CW laser design, EML design, and owned 6-inch InP capacity.** Capital-intensive, yield-secret, decade-long learning curve. Global InP epitaxy is concentrated in a handful of houses; Coherent is one of very few module players not hostage to them. With a reported 30%+ EML supply gap [T3], owning epitaxy is not a margin story — it is an **allocation** story. Assemblers cannot ship what they cannot source. **This layer is the moat, and it is genuinely wide.**
- **Integration and assembly (thin, eroding): module packaging and transceiver assembly.** Chinese specialists demonstrably do this faster, cheaper, and at better net margins. Eoptolink #7 → #3 in one year is the proof. **There is no durable moat here, and Coherent should not be valued as though there is.**

**Why "emerging" rather than "durable" overall:** the CPO/ELS franchise that justified the re-rating is *contracted but not yet revenue*. Scale-out CPO revenue was guided to begin H2 CY2026 — roughly now, unproven. Scale-up is a H2 CY2027 story. The $2B is banked; the franchise is not.

**The single most likely way the moat erodes.** NVIDIA (or Broadcom) commoditizes the ELS socket by design. The mechanism is already visible and requires no new development: NVIDIA wrote **two** ~$2B checks — Coherent's and Lumentum's — both explicitly **non-exclusive**. That is textbook dual-sourcing to guarantee supply while preventing supplier pricing power. The ELS is deliberately architected as a **pluggable, standardized, faceplate module** — precisely the form factor that invites third- and fourth-source entry, and AAOI and Sivers are already entering. On this reading, Coherent's $2B is best understood as **capacity pre-funding, not a moat**. If ELSFP standardizes under OCP and Chinese vendors qualify into it the way they did with pluggables, Coherent owns the die and rents the module — high volume, compressing margin, and the identical trap the transceiver business already fell into once.

## 5. Technological moat & competing technologies

### Level 1 — competing approaches to the same problem

| Approach | How it works | Champions | Trade-off vs. Coherent's DSP-pluggable base case |
|---|---|---|---|
| **DSP-retimed pluggables (800G/1.6T/3.2T)** | Full DSP in a faceplate module; re-times and equalizes the signal | Innolight, Eoptolink, **Coherent**, Lumentum; Marvell/Broadcom DSPs | The baseline. Highest power per bit (~15–20W+ at 1.6T), but maximum flexibility, field-replaceable, mature yield, established RMA |
| **LPO (linear-drive pluggable)** | Removes the DSP; switch SerDes drives the optics linearly | Eoptolink, Credo, Marvell; broad hyperscaler interest | ~30–50% lower power, lower latency and cost, **keeps serviceability**. Loses link margin and reach; harder interop; needs tight host-optics co-design |
| **LRO (linear receive optics)** | DSP retained on TX only, linear RX | Credo, Marvell | Half of LPO's power saving but far easier to qualify — the pragmatic bridge |
| **CPO (co-packaged optics)** | Optical engines mounted on the switch ASIC substrate; **lasers stay external** | **NVIDIA, Broadcom**, TSMC (COUPE), Marvell (via Celestial AI) | 3.5x lower interconnect power and ~70% optical power reduction claimed. **Loses field-replaceability**, compounds multi-engine yield risk, couples optics thermally to a hot ASIC, complicates RMA |
| **NPO (near-package optics)** | Engines on the same board but not on the ASIC substrate | Ciena/Nubis; OCP work | Most of CPO's power win while retaining some serviceability — widely treated as the industry hedge |
| **DAC / AEC (copper)** | Passive or active electrical cable | Credo, Amphenol, Astera Labs | Near-zero optical cost and power, but reach-limited (~1–2m passive). The copper reach wall is exactly what forces optics into scale-up |
| **OCS (optical circuit switching)** | Switches whole light paths; no per-packet electrical conversion | **Google**, **Coherent (LCoS)**, Lumentum/Calient/Polatis (MEMS) | Not a transceiver substitute — a topology layer. Large power win for reconfigurable fabrics; slow switching, no packet inspection |

### What the industry is actually converging on

**It is not converging on one thing, and that is the single most important framing correction in this report.** The evidence:

- **2026–2027 volume remains pluggables**, with LPO/LRO taking the power-sensitive share.
- **Broadcom deliberately ships both** CPO platforms *and* pure electrical ASICs for pluggables/LPO — a multi-path hedge by the best-informed participant, not a bet.
- **CPO in 2026 is announcements and early shipments, not volume.** Cignal AI's Q1 2026 update (April 2026) is blunt: there is a great deal of noise but nothing shipping in volume yet [T]. NVIDIA Quantum-X Photonics (InfiniBand) reached commercial availability in early 2026; Spectrum-X Ethernet Photonics is slated for H2 2026; Broadcom's Tomahawk 6-Davisson (102.4 Tb/s) began shipping in 2026; TSMC COUPE entered mass production in April 2026. All true — none of it means millions of ports. Notably, The Next Platform's October 2025 headline on Broadcom's third-generation CPO effort was that the third time would be the charm, an honest signal that generations one and two did not convert. [T]

**Convergence verdict:** scale-out switch ports migrate to CPO gradually from 2027, reaching material volume in 2028–2030; scale-up (GPU-to-GPU) optics is the larger prize and is a 2027+ story; **pluggables and LPO remain the majority of unit volume through at least 2028.** Anyone claiming a clean 2026–2027 cutover is ahead of the evidence — and so is anyone claiming CPO is a 2029 non-event.

### The defensibility assessment

**Genuinely proprietary:** InP crystal growth and epitaxy; high-power CW DFB and EML die design; the 6-inch InP transition (>4x devices at <half the cost — a quantified, hard-to-replicate cost advantage); VCSEL fabs; LCoS optical circuit switching. Replicating owned InP epitaxy requires roughly a decade of yield learning and hundreds of millions in capital — this is the real barrier.

**Orchestration/integration on third-party components:** module packaging, DSP integration (bought from Marvell/Broadcom), silicon photonics die (foundry-dependent), chassis assembly. Switching costs here are qualification-cycle-length (6–18 months), not structural.

**The laser-content question, honestly unresolved.** NVIDIA's Quantum-X uses **36 laser inputs to feed 288 data links** — roughly one laser per eight links, far fewer laser *units* per port than pluggables, where each 800G module carries 4–8 EMLs. But CPO lasers are **much higher power** (~400mW/channel at 1310nm; Lumentum has demonstrated 800mW), meaning larger die, harder yield and more epitaxial area per laser. There is also a technology shift: pluggables use modulated **EMLs**, CPO uses **CW DFBs** with modulation moved onto the silicon photonics die. **No credible source nets this out to a per-bit InP wafer demand figure.** Any claim that CPO is InP-accretive or InP-dilutive per bit should be treated as unverified — including claims made in either direction by interested parties.

**Verdict: technological moat DURABLE at the die layer, EMERGING (optionality) at the module/CPO layer, and ABSENT at the assembly layer.** The correct way to hold this name is as a bet on the die, not on the box.

## 6. Secular / thematic positioning

Coherent's exposure to AI infrastructure is **first-derivative on volume but second-derivative on the architecture decision** — an unusual and important combination.

**First-derivative:** revenue scales directly with the number of high-speed optical ports deployed in AI datacenters, which scales with GPU deployments, which scales with hyperscaler capex. There is no intermediating bet. D&C revenue grew 41% YoY in a period when the four largest hyperscalers guided aggregate 2026 capex to ~$725B, up ~77% from ~$410B in 2025 [T3 — sell-side tallies of individually-guided figures]. This is as direct as picks-and-shovels exposure gets without being the GPU itself.

**Second-derivative — and this is the differentiating feature:** Coherent is *not* exposed to which architecture wins. Pluggable, LPO, LRO, CPO and NPO all require InP light sources from the same fab. The company is short the *timing* of the transition (because it is spending capex against a specific curve) but roughly **neutral on the outcome**. This is why the market's decision to sell Coherent on a CPO-delay report is analytically confused — the delay changes the mix between two products Coherent sells, not whether it sells them.

**Where the exposure is genuinely second-derivative and weaker:** OCS (a topology-layer bet dependent on hyperscalers adopting reconfigurable fabrics), multi-rail (revenue guided to start 2027, est. $2B+ opportunity), and thermal solutions (revenue guided late 2027). These are real optionality but contribute nothing today and should carry no weight in a current-year valuation.

**The counter-exposure:** the Industrial segment (25% of revenue, −16% YoY) is exposed to a weak global industrial capex cycle and provides negative thematic torque. Management is actively divesting into it.

A momentum holder anchoring on "net income up 4x" is misreading a portfolio action as an operating turn. Both distortions belong in §3's fundamental bucket.

## 8. Risks

### Pre-mortem — the single most likely fundamental mechanism by which this equity is impaired

**Not bankruptcy — the balance sheet effectively rules that out (net debt $776M against ~$7B of revenue, no maturity until FY2029). The realistic pre-mortem is a margin-structure collapse via ELS commoditization, and it runs as follows:**

NVIDIA has deliberately architected the external laser source as a **standardized, pluggable, faceplate module** and has funded **two** suppliers with identical non-exclusive $2B agreements. Coherent spends heavily through 2027 to build InP and ELS capacity against NVIDIA's committed volumes. Meanwhile the ELSFP form factor standardizes through OCP. AAOI and Sivers — already entering — qualify, followed by Chinese vendors who historically compress module pricing 20–30% per generation. By 2028 the ELS socket has four to six qualified sources and Coherent is bidding for volume it has already built fixed capacity to serve. Gross margin on the CPO-adjacent business compresses toward the assembly-layer economics that Innolight and Eoptolink already impose in pluggables. Coherent ends up **owning the laser die and renting the module** — high revenue, structurally lower margin, and a permanently lower multiple. The stock does not go to zero; it goes to a mid-teens multiple on lower-quality earnings, which from a $54B market cap is a ~50–60% permanent impairment.

*The named mechanism is deliberate customer-driven commoditization of the socket Coherent is spending to win — not "competition" generically.*

### Named and specific risks

1. **Customer concentration — quantified but stale and unnamed.** FY2025: **two customers at 12% and 10% of consolidated revenue**, purchasing primarily from the former Networking segment; FY2024 had *one* customer at 10%; FY2023 one at 10%. **Concentration is increasing.** Coherent does not name them and explicitly declines customer-level disaggregation. The newest datapoint is ~11 months old and predates the entire D&C surge and the NVIDIA agreement. The 10-Q concedes the NVIDIA arrangement "could affect revenue concentration, gross margin, and capital expenditures as volumes ramp" without quantifying anything. **Expect a materially higher figure in the FY2026 10-K in ~4 weeks.** [P]
2. **NVIDIA as a triple-vector single point of failure.** It is simultaneously the largest disclosed commercial counterparty, a ~4% shareholder, and the architect of the roadmap that determines whether pluggables are a growth asset or a melting ice cube. If NVIDIA re-weights toward Lumentum under the identical non-exclusive contract, Coherent's volumes fall **without any hyperscaler capex cut occurring at all.**
3. **The inventory conversion risk — the fastest-acting near-term risk.** $2,126.8M of inventory (+48%), WIP $1,238M, against a demand curve controlled by counterparties, with capex guided to increase again sequentially. A two-quarter pushout converts this into idle capacity plus writedown risk on a 37.7% GAAP gross margin base.
4. **Second-derivative credit exposure with no early warning.** Coherent has no privity with CoreWeave or OpenAI, but NVIDIA and the OEMs do. CoreWeave has borrowed $40B+, mostly high-yield, with ~$29B of total liabilities and $4.2B due in 2026 to refinance, and closed a $3.1B GPU-collateralized facility backed by two non-investment-grade customer contracts [T]. A neocloud funding failure reaches Coherent as an order cut through NVIDIA — invisible in Coherent's own receivables until it is already happening.
5. **Open BIS/EAR export-control inquiry, unquantified.** 10-Q Note 11 discloses active discussions with the Bureau of Industry and Security regarding past product sales, with requests for additional information and no determination received. Coherent states it is **unable to estimate a range of loss**. China is 11% of Q3 revenue ($206M). [P]
6. **Share loss to Chinese assemblers.** Structural, ongoing, pre-dating the sell-off, and the least-discussed genuine fundamental negative. See §4.
7. **Goodwill and intangibles are 43% of total assets** ($4.40B goodwill + $2.96B intangibles). The segment reorganization forced a fresh impairment test (passed), but a demand shock would put a large non-cash charge in play — and the Industrial segment has already absorbed $85M (FY25 Q4) plus $20.1M (FY26) of held-for-sale impairments.
8. **Insurance disclosure is entirely absent.** No insurance note in the FY2025 10-K or the Q3 FY26 10-Q — no coverage limits, no self-insurance reserves, no D&O detail, no property or business-interruption discussion. Given a concentrated fab footprint and explicit environmental/hazardous-materials risk factors, **coverage adequacy is unverifiable from primary filings.** This is a real disclosure gap, not an omission from this report.
9. **FX hedging paused.** The balance-sheet hedging program has been **paused indefinitely since 2024-09-30**. [P]
10. **Supply chain:** dependence on limited sources for strategic materials, plus named exposure to rare earth minerals and noble gases, tariffs, and export restrictions. [P — FY2025 10-K Item 1A]
11. **Governance:** Bain (BCPE Watson) retains the right to nominate one director while holding ≥25% of its originally issued shares on an as-converted basis; its designee **Stephen Pagliuca** sits on the board. Bain has been reducing — Bloomberg reported a $2.3B block sought in February 2026 and a subsequent SC 13D/A indicates a full exit [T]. NVIDIA has **no** disclosed board seat or governance rights.

## 10. Sector bottleneck / structural analysis

**The binding constraint in AI datacenter buildout has moved three times in three years, and identifying where it sits now is the whole game.**

- **2023–24: GPU supply.** Resolved by TSMC capacity and CoWoS expansion.
- **2025: interconnect bandwidth.** Copper hit its reach wall; optics became mandatory for scale-out and increasingly for scale-up. This is the transition that took Coherent from ~$100 to ~$440.
- **2026: two constraints simultaneously — laser/EML supply, and power.**

**The laser constraint is where Coherent monetizes.** Trade estimates put the EML supply gap above 30% [T3], with wafer fabs running at double their prior run-rate and still short. In that condition, owning epitaxy is not a margin advantage — it is an **allocation** advantage. Assemblers cannot ship what they cannot source, which is why Coherent's InP doubling landing a quarter early is more thesis-relevant than its accounting effect suggests. **This is the bottleneck Coherent solves, and it is the strongest structural argument in the entire thesis.**

**The power constraint is where the whole theme is vulnerable, and Coherent does not solve it.** The binding limit on datacenter deployment has shifted to transformers, switchgear and grid interconnect, with equipment waits reported up to five years and estimates that 30–50% of 2026 US datacenter projects will be delayed or cancelled [T3 — consultancy research, **not issuer-filed; directionally useful, not citable as fact**]. Only about a third of the ~12GW 2026 pipeline is reported to be actively under construction. **Coherent's optics cannot ship into a datacenter that cannot be energized.** This is the mechanism by which strong hyperscaler capex *guidance* could still coexist with weak *actual* optics orders — and it is the most plausible route to the bear case (a) without any hyperscaler ever cutting a published number.

**Where Coherent sits:** it monetizes the constraint it solves (light) and is exposed to the constraint it does not (power). Its OCS product is a partial hedge — optical circuit switching offers a large power reduction versus electrical switching (Google's Apollo architecture claims ~95% per-switch power reduction) and therefore sells *into* the power constraint rather than being blocked by it. That is a genuine, if currently small, structural offset.

## 12. Asymmetry verdict

**Floor.** Genuinely earnings-supported rather than structural. Net debt ~$776M against ~$7B of revenue and ~$2.4B of cash plus short-term investments; no maturity before 7/1/2029; no converts; no ATM; covenant headroom (0.5x against a 4.00x maximum); an undrawn $668M revolver; and a business generating positive GAAP net income. **Coherent cannot be forced into dilutive financing at a distressed price** — the failure mode that makes most sub-scale optics names uninvestable in a drawdown. A realistic floor is a mid-to-high-20s multiple on materially reduced earnings, i.e. the $150–230 bear band, and reaching the bottom of that requires *both* a demand reset and a structural margin reset.

**Ceiling.** Bounded by share dynamics and by module economics rather than by TAM. Even in the bull case, Coherent is growing at roughly a third to a half the rate of Innolight and Eoptolink, and the CPO TAM on independent estimates ($8–10B by 2030) is roughly half the company's own claim. This is not an unbounded optionality story.

**Verdict: a moderately asymmetric bet with a genuine, balance-sheet-supported floor — but it is *not* a low-floor/high-ceiling lottery ticket, and it should not be sized as one.** It is better described as **a quality-compounder-at-a-reset-price with a live timing risk.** The distinguishing feature versus its peer group is the floor: AAOI and similar names carry solvency-shaped bear cases, while Coherent's bear case is a multiple compression on a profitable franchise. The asymmetry comes from balance-sheet quality, not from the payoff shape of the upside.

**The honest qualifier:** the asymmetry is favourable *if and only if* the inventory converts. Roughly $2.1B of inventory and a nine-month $537M free-cash-flow deficit is the hinge. If that converts, the floor is as described. If it does not, the floor drops because an impairment plus idle capacity resets the earnings base that defines the floor — and floor-and-ceiling analysis conducted before that resolves is provisional. **13 August is when the asymmetry becomes knowable rather than estimated.**

At the $426.89 peak the multiple was ~66x the same annualized guidance, which required both the earnings growth **and** the CPO TAM arriving on the 2027 schedule. **The de-rate from ~66x to ~43x is almost exactly the removal of the CPO-timing premium, and nothing else.** That arithmetic is the most precise available answer to whether the fundamentals changed: the market removed a specific, identifiable premium for an event that got pushed out, and left the base business multiple broadly intact.

That is not a valuation model, and NVIDIA's motives are strategic rather than purely financial — it was buying capacity and priority access, not seeking a return — but as a sanity check on whether $277.60 represents distress or exuberance, it is worth more than any multiple comparison in this section.

**Verdict: the multiple is no longer stretched, but it is not compressed either — it is roughly fair for the disclosed fundamentals and cheap only if the growth durability holds.** The stock is not a value opportunity at 43x. It is a growth business whose price has returned to something defensible after a narrative overshoot in both directions.

## 15. Investment thesis (synthesis)

**Reason 1 — Coherent owns the one input every competing architecture requires.** Pluggables, LPO, LRO, CPO and NPO all need indium phosphide light. Coherent grows its own InP, runs its own epitaxy, and has moved to 6-inch wafers yielding >4x the devices at <half the cost, in full production at Sherman with capacity doubling delivered a quarter early. In a market with a reported 30%+ EML supply gap, that is an allocation advantage, not merely a margin advantage. *Counterweight:* the moat is in the die, not the module. Module assembly has no durable moat, and Coherent is losing share in it — Eoptolink went #7→#3 in a year while Coherent's datacenter revenue grew at roughly a third the rate of its two closest rivals. **The company earns a wide-moat multiple on one layer and a commodity multiple on the other; the blended result is "moderate."**

**Reason 2 — The best-informed possible buyer validated the position with $2B in cash and a commitment through 2030.** NVIDIA bought 7,788,161 shares at $256.80 plus a non-exclusive multi-billion purchase commitment and access to five CPO product families. This is not analyst speculation about Coherent's CPO relevance — it is a filed, executed, capital-backed answer. *Counterweight:* NVIDIA wrote the **same** ~$2B cheque to Lumentum on the same day, on explicitly non-exclusive terms. That is deliberate dual-sourcing to secure supply while denying either supplier pricing power. The $2B is best read as **capacity pre-funding, not a moat** — and it makes NVIDIA a single point of failure across three vectors at once (largest counterparty, ~4% holder, and architect of the roadmap that sets Coherent's terminal value).

**Reason 3 — The balance sheet was genuinely rebuilt, and it changes the shape of the downside.** Leverage 2.1x → 0.5x in a year; $502M of *voluntary* term-loan prepayment; the entire $2.5B Series B preferred converted and retired; no converts, no ATM, no maturity before 7/1/2029; $2.4B of cash and short-term investments; S&P upgrade to 'BB'. This means the bear case is a de-rating of a profitable franchise rather than a solvency event — Coherent cannot be forced to raise equity into weakness. *Counterweight:* it was rebuilt substantially with *purchased* capital — $2.0B from NVIDIA and $437M from divestitures — while operations generated $10.1M of cash in nine months against $547M of capex. The balance sheet is currently being carried by a strategic investor and asset sales rather than by the business.

**Reason 4 — The de-rate removed a specific, identifiable premium rather than repricing the business.** The multiple went from ~66x to ~43x annualized guided earnings; that compression maps almost exactly onto the removal of the CPO-timing premium after the 9 June SemiAnalysis report. Over the same window, no guidance was revised, no contract lost, no 8-K filed, and the sell-side raised targets. *Counterweight:* the premium **deserved** to come out. The company's own ">$15B" CPO TAM is 1.5–2x independent estimates from Yole ($8.1B by 2030) and LightCounting (~$10B by 2030), and its scope is undefined. A multiple built on a promotional TAM arriving in 2027 is correctly lower when the arrival moves to 2028–29. The market's *direction* was right even if its reasoning — selling a pluggable franchise on news that pluggables will last longer — was confused.

**What has to be true.** Hyperscaler capex holds near its guided trajectory through 2027; the $2.1B inventory converts into shipped revenue on roughly the guided schedule; Coherent defends share against Innolight and Eoptolink well enough that the die-layer moat is not offset by module-layer erosion; and the external-laser-source socket does not commoditize into a four-source race. **The first two are testable within four weeks. The last two take three years.**

## 16. Review of the investor's submitted premise

*A formal written thesis was not submitted, but a specific analytical premise was — that the CPO-driven sell-off has not changed Coherent's fundamentals. Per the standing rule that a wrong premise gets corrected before it gets answered, that premise is assessed directly here rather than written around.*

**(a) Confirmed correct.** The core instinct holds and is well-supported by primary sources. **No operating fundamental deteriorated between the 3 June peak and 17 July.** Concretely: guidance was never revised ($1.91–2.05B revenue, $1.52–1.72 non-GAAP EPS, 39–41% gross margin all still stand); the last reported quarter grew 21% with the lead segment at +41% and a 25.5% segment margin; backlog extends into calendar 2028 with no disclosed cancellation; leverage is 0.5x versus 2.1x a year earlier; and **only two SEC filings were made in the entire drawdown window — a 13G/A and a single Form 4 for tax withholding.** No 8-K. Five of the six largest moves since the peak had no company-specific cause, and on the 2 July −9.6% session a broker *raised* its target to $435. Short interest of ~3.34% rules out a short-driven mechanic. The instinct that this is sentiment rather than fundamentals is **correct, and the evidence for it is stronger than most such claims.**

**(b) Corrections and refinements — three, and the third is the important one.**

1. **Right conclusion, but the CPO mechanism is backwards from how it is usually stated.** The bear catalyst was not "CPO will kill pluggables" — it was the *opposite*: SemiAnalysis argued CPO is **delayed** to 2028–29. That is **neutral-to-positive** for Coherent's largest current revenue line, because every quarter CPO does not ramp is a quarter in which 800G/1.6T pluggables remain the only option. The market sold Coherent on news that extends the life of its core franchise. So the defence is not "CPO isn't a threat" — it is sharper than that: *Coherent sits on both sides, supplying the external laser source, high-power CW lasers, fiber attach units, isolators and TECs into CPO, while every architecture consumes the same InP capacity it is expanding.*
2. **But "fundamentals unchanged" does not mean "the de-rate was irrational."** The multiple fell from ~66x to ~43x annualized guided earnings, and that compression maps almost precisely onto the removal of a CPO-timing premium. A terminal-value story moving out a year *should* reduce the multiple even when current EPS is untouched. **Fundamentals unchanged and the sell-off justified are both true simultaneously** — they are statements about different things (earnings versus the discount rate applied to optionality). Treating the drawdown as pure irrationality would be the mirror-image error to the one the sellers made.
3. **One thing genuinely did change, and it is not what anyone is trading.** **Operating cash flow for the nine months to 3/31/26 was $10.1M, versus $503.3M in the prior-year period**, on net earnings that *rose* to $551M. Inventories consumed $699M (WIP $824M → $1,238M) and capex nearly doubled to $547M, producing a ~**−$537M free cash flow** for the period. This is defensible as a pre-ramp build — opex is scaling below revenue, gross margin is expanding, and it is asset-building rather than structural burn — but it is a real deterioration in cash conversion and it means the balance sheet is presently carried by NVIDIA's $2B and $437M of divestiture proceeds rather than by operations. **Notably, this was disclosed on 6 May — before the June peak.** The market bought to $440 with this number already public and is now selling for unrelated reasons. Both sides are trading the narrative rather than the filings.

**(c) Net assessment.** The premise is **substantially correct on the question asked and incomplete on the question that matters more.** The judgment that the sell-off is sentiment-driven rather than fundamentals-driven is well-founded, better-founded than usual, and this report documents it in detail. But two refinements should change how the conclusion is held. First, "the fundamentals haven't changed" is a statement about *earnings*, and the drawdown was mostly a repricing of *optionality* — so the two are not in contradiction, and the de-rate does not require the market to have been wrong. Second, and more importantly, **the correct thing to worry about is not CPO at all.** It is the $2.1B inventory build and whether it converts, and secondarily the multi-year share drift toward Innolight and Eoptolink. Both are visible in primary sources today; neither is in the current narrative. **An investor who takes reassurance from the CPO analysis and stops there will have gotten the right answer to the wrong question.** The single most useful action is to read the 13 August cash-flow statement before the revenue headline.

## 18. Final thoughts & conclusion

**Pulling the threads together.** Coherent is a genuinely good business at a now-defensible price with one unresolved operational question and one unresolved structural question. The business model is sound and correctly positioned — it sells the one input (InP light) that every competing interconnect architecture requires, which makes it unusually indifferent to the pluggable-versus-CPO argument that just took a third off its market value. The catalysts are dense and near-dated. The re-rate map is currently dominated by a transient sentiment/technical bucket, which is why the price moved without the fundamentals moving. The moat is real but must be held in two pieces: wide and durable at the laser die, thin and eroding at module assembly, which is why "moderate/emerging" is the honest verdict rather than the "wide" the narrative implies. The financials are strong on the income statement, transformed on the balance sheet, and genuinely weak on cash conversion. The risks are concentrated in one counterparty and one inventory balance. Valuation has returned from ~66x to ~43x forward guided earnings — no longer stretched, not yet cheap. Sentiment is a battleground in which neither side appears to be reading the cash-flow statement. The asymmetry is favourable and balance-sheet-supported, but conditional on inventory conversion.

**Your edge — what do you believe that the crowd doesn't?**
The differentiated view available here is narrow but genuine, and it is **not** "the sell-off was overdone" — that is a consensus-adjacent view already reflected in a ~$380–384 average price target. The actual variant perception is this: **the market has the CPO mechanism backwards, and is worried about the wrong risk.** The 9 June catalyst was a *delay* to CPO, which extends the life of Coherent's largest revenue line while deferring a business that independent analysts size at half what management claims. Meanwhile Coherent supplies into CPO regardless, and every architecture consumes the same InP capacity. So the thing that was sold is roughly neutral-to-positive, while the thing that genuinely deteriorated — operating cash flow collapsing to $10.1M on a $699M inventory build — was disclosed on 6 May and is being traded by nobody in either direction. **The edge is holding both halves at once: the CPO fear is misplaced, *and* the complacency about cash conversion is also misplaced.** If your view is simply "good company, oversold, buy the dip," you do not have an edge — you are agreeing with the sell-side at a 38% discount to its target and betting that sentiment reverts for no particular reason. Be honest about which of those two you actually hold.

**Your tripwire — what would prove you wrong?**
Pre-commit to this, in advance: **on the FY2026 Q4 report (13 August 2026), if operating cash flow for the full fiscal year does not inflect materially positive — specifically, if FY2026 OCF comes in below roughly $300M against ~$700M of capex, or if inventory fails to decline from $2,126.8M — the ramp thesis is not working and the position must be re-underwritten or exited.** That is a single, observable, dated, binary test on a number the company must publish. It is deliberately chosen over a revenue miss because revenue is guided and largely pre-sold, whereas cash conversion is where a demand pushout actually shows up first. Decide the action now, before the print, rather than rationalizing an explanation afterwards. A secondary tripwire: if the FY2026 10-K discloses any single customer above ~20% of revenue, the name has converted from a diversified merchant supplier into a single-counterparty story and warrants a materially smaller position regardless of the fundamentals.

**Time-horizon fit.** The thesis needs **two to four years** to resolve fully, because the load-bearing question — whether the ELS socket commoditizes — cannot be answered before 2028. But the near-term gates are unusually dense: the hyperscaler capex commentary lands within about two weeks of this report, the Q4 print on 13 August, first disclosed CPO revenue in H2 CY2026, and the NVIDIA lock-up around 2 September. **This is a poor fit for a horizon shorter than a few quarters** — implied volatility at the 99.6th percentile and three levered ETFs guarantee violent noise — and a reasonable fit for a two-to-four-year holding period, provided the holder can tolerate 10% single-session moves without being forced out.

**Conviction-appropriate sizing.** This is **not** a binary or single-counterparty bet in the way a development-stage name is — there is real revenue, real profit, real backlog and a genuinely strong balance sheet, and the bear case is a de-rate rather than a wipeout. But it **is** meaningfully single-counterparty-*influenced*: NVIDIA is simultaneously the largest disclosed commercial relationship, a ~4% holder, and the architect of the roadmap that sets terminal value, while the two 10%+ customers remain unnamed and concentration is rising. That argues for a position sized below what the balance-sheet quality alone would justify, and for treating the 13 August print as a genuine sizing checkpoint rather than a formality.

**What kills the thesis fastest.** Not the pre-mortem — ELS commoditization is the most *severe* risk but it plays out over years. The **fastest-acting** invalidation is the collision of the highest-impact near-term gate with the largest balance-sheet exposure: **hyperscaler capex commentary in late July signalling digestion, followed by a 13 August print showing the $2.1B inventory failing to convert.** That sequence would turn a defensible pre-ramp build into an impairment plus idle capacity within a single quarter, and would reset the earnings base that currently defines the floor. It is the one path that damages the bear case's floor rather than merely compressing the multiple, and it could be fully visible within four weeks of this report's date.

**Where the price sits relative to the thesis.** **Roughly fairly priced, with a modest positive skew.** At ~43x annualized Q4 guidance the market is paying for 25–35% earnings growth over roughly three years with no margin reversal and no share loss — demanding, but not the near-term CPO windfall it was pricing at $426.89 (~66x). This is neither a distressed price nor an exuberant one. It is not a value opportunity, and anyone framing a 37% drawdown as "deep value" on a 43x multiple is anchoring to the chart rather than the multiple.
