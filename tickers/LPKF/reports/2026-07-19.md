## Executive summary — investment thesis

**What it is, and what the debate actually is.** LPKF makes laser systems. Four segments — Electronics, Development, Solar, Welding — generate ~€115m of cyclical, barely-profitable revenue and are, for equity purposes, ballast. The entire equity story is a fifth thing that is not yet a segment: **LIDE** (Laser-Induced Deep Etching), a two-step process that modifies glass with a picosecond laser and then wet-etches the modified track, producing through-glass vias without the microcracking that kills yield in brittle substrates. If glass-core substrates become the packaging standard for AI accelerators, LIDE is the picks-and-shovels way to own it. **The debate is not quality and it is not price. It is duration** — how long the option has to run before it pays, and whether the balance sheet reaches the payoff.

**The three hard facts that anchor the snapshot.**

1. **Zero LIDE series orders exist.** Management told the 4 June 2026 AGM it expected first LIDE *production* orders "in the current second quarter." Q2 ended 30 June with none; the shares fell **28% on 1 July** `[T]`. LPKF's own Q1 language is "concrete discussions with several customers regarding orders for initial production systems" — discussions, not orders `[P]`.
2. **Management's own horizon is 2029, not 2027.** At the same AGM, management guided **ramp not before 2027 and high-volume output only from 2029** `[T]`. FY2026 guidance **explicitly excludes** Advanced Semiconductor Packaging volume orders, on the stated grounds that timing is gated by **qualification of downstream process steps, not by LIDE** `[P]`. That is management conceding the bottleneck sits outside its control.
3. **Cash is €5.8m against a ~€7m quarterly EBIT burn.** Cash fell €4.3m in Q1 2026 alone; the equity ratio dropped 5.4 points in one quarter (73.2% → 67.8%). The backstop is a syndicated facility giving **€15.5m of drawable liquidity**, three-year term, **resizable only with the five (unnamed) lenders' consent** `[P]`.

**Three reasons to own — each with its counterweight.**

- **The IP is real and has been tested.** The core European LIDE patent was granted in 2022, an anonymous opposition was **fully rejected** with claims intact at the October 2024 Opposition Division hearing, and the appeal window lapsed 23 February 2025 `[P]`. A patent someone paid to attack and failed to break is stronger evidence than an unchallenged one. US family members (US11065716, US11377387, US11383972) extend coverage, with priority tracing to ~2017–18 and implied expiry ~2037–38. **Counterweight:** patents protect claims, not a physics category — and the open literature is now publishing the process window (Bessel-beam TGV formation, etchant-selectivity/taper studies), which is precisely the roadmap a competitor needs to design around the claim set. Per-jurisdiction status in **KR/CN/TW — where the competitive risk actually lives — was not verified** (Caveat 5).
- **Design-in breadth is genuinely wide.** CEO Klaus Fiedler claims **">80% of major global players have selected our equipment"** for validation/MP preparation `[T]`. If directionally true, LPKF is on the learning curve at nearly every serious program. **Counterweight:** this is a *tool-count-at-R&D-lines* metric, not revenue share and not a production commitment — and the Q2 miss is the proof of the distinction. **80% design-in has produced zero series orders.** The claim is also unverifiable as stated and appears to conflate R&D tool purchases with production wins.
- **The funded end of the supply chain got healthier.** SKC recapitalized in May 2026 with a **KRW 1.1671tn** rights offering, putting **KRW 589.6bn directly into Absolics** and cutting group leverage from ~230% to ~129–140% D/E; Absolics' Covington, Georgia fab is sampling to **AMD** and reportedly near qualification, with AWS also testing `[T]`. Absolics carries up to **$175m** of US government support (CHIPS + R&D grants). **Counterweight:** none of that money is contractually LPKF's, and this is the one node where LPKF's content is actively contradicted — reporting indicates Absolics contracted a different vendor for part of its process flow (single-source, unverified — excluded from this report's conclusions, see Caveat 7).

**What has to be true.** All of: (a) glass-core packaging industrializes on roughly the 2027–2029 timeline rather than slipping to 2030+; (b) LIDE converts pilot placements into series orders *before* the balance sheet forces a raise; (c) LPKF holds the first HVM reference socket against Philoptics, which is expanding tool count per customer at the same accounts; and (d) the eventual raise, if one comes, is not executed at a de-rated price into a thin float. These are conjunctive, and (b) is on a shorter clock than (a).

**Bottom line.** The stock has **halved in nineteen days** — from a derived ~€30.00 on 30 June to €15.00 today — and the sell-side sits at €10–15, at or below spot. That is not a market that needs convincing the technology works; it is a market repricing *when* it pays and *who* funds the wait. The prior underwriting of this name framed the question as "does the order land on 23 July." The evidence assembled here says that is the wrong question: management has already told the market the ramp is 2027 and high volume is 2029, which converts this from a timing trade into a **financing-duration** problem. The 23 July H1 report, four days out, is the immediate gate — but it resolves the near-term catalyst, not the thesis.

LPKF sells capital equipment — laser systems — into four reported segments. FY2025 revenue was **€115.3m, −6.2% YoY**, landing at the low end of guidance, while adjusted EBIT improved from **€0.1m to €0.8m** on cost reduction rather than volume `[P]`.

⚠️ **A segment revenue/margin table is not presented, because the split could not be sourced to primary at the required granularity in this run** (Caveat 3). The above is directional commentary from the FY2025 release, not a quantitative split — a gap that a normal filing-agent pass would have closed.

**Position in the value chain — what LPKF is not.** LPKF is **not** a glass maker (that is Corning, Schott, AGC, NEG, who together control >90% of low-CTE glass formulation and melting), **not** a substrate maker (Absolics, Samsung Electro-Mechanics, Ibiden, Unimicron), and **not** a packaging house. It sells **one process step** — via formation — into a line that also requires glass supply, metallization/RDL, inspection, and assembly. This is the single most important structural fact about the name: LPKF's revenue is a derivative of someone else's capex decision, and its own guidance concedes that the gate is **downstream qualification it does not control**.

The order book tells the story of a company whose base business is shrinking while it waits: **order intake €91.6m (−19.9%)** and **backlog €27.0m, down €23.9m YoY** `[P]`. Q1 2026 order intake of €24.1m at a **1.4× book-to-bill** is the counter-signal — but on revenue of only €17.1m, a 1.4× book-to-bill is a comment on how far revenue has fallen, not on how strong demand is.

## 4. Technological moat & competing technologies

**Level 1 — competing approaches to through-glass-via formation.**

| Approach | How it works | Champions | Trade-off vs. LIDE |
|---|---|---|---|
| **LIDE** (subject) | Picosecond laser modifies glass through full thickness in a single pulse; ~10% HF wet etch selectively removes the modified track. No melting, no ablation | LPKF (Vitrion) | Baseline. No microcracks, no HAZ, high aspect ratio; batch etch decouples throughput from via count. Requires HF wet-chemistry infrastructure at the customer |
| **Direct laser drilling / ablation** | Laser removes material pulse-by-pulse (percussion/trepan) | Han's Laser (FLEE-TGV), Coherent, Trumpf, Disco, Via Mechanics | Serial per via → throughput degrades as via count rises; heat-affected zone, microcracking, debris/recast; taper control harder |
| **Laser-assisted / selective laser etching (SLE) variants** | Same two-step logic — beam-shaped modification then chemical etch | Multiple Asian entrants; academic groups | **The genuine IP battleground.** Functionally converges on LIDE; published work shows etchant choice governs taper and selectivity — i.e. the process window is being reverse-engineered in the open literature |
| **Photosensitive glass** (APEX/Foturan class) | Litho exposure → ceramization → isotropic wet etch | 3D Glass Solutions, Schott (Foturan) | Reaches AR >10 and <100µm vias, but locks the line to a proprietary specialty glass — kills the cheap-standard-glass economics the substrate case depends on |
| **Plasma / DRIE glass etch** | Fluorine-radical directional etch | MEMS toolmakers; Lam adjacent | Very slow on glass, poor mask selectivity, high capex/wafer; fine for MEMS, uneconomic at panel scale |
| **Mechanical / abrasive** | Sandblast, ultrasonic, micro-drill | Legacy PCB/interposer | Cheap capex; vias >100µm, poor taper, edge chipping. Non-competitive for AI packaging |
| **ECDM / SACE** | Spark discharge in electrolyte | Research only | Poor repeatability and yield; not industrialized |

**Where the industry is converging, and why it cuts both ways.** The industry is converging on **laser modification plus selective wet etch** — the LIDE family — because it is the only approach that simultaneously delivers high aspect ratio at sub-50µm via diameter, near-zero microcracking (the dominant yield/reliability killer in brittle glass), and a batch etch step that decouples throughput from via count as densities climb into the hundreds of thousands per panel. Independent metrology work corroborates that **via quality and taper control, not formation speed, is the yield gate** `[T]`.

That convergence is the moat's central ambiguity: **LPKF invented the category, and the category is now the industry's default approach.** That invites re-implementation *around* the patents rather than avoidance of the physics.

**Defensibility assessment.** Genuinely proprietary: the granted EPO patent (survived full opposition, claims intact, no appeal), the US family, and accumulated recipe/etch-window know-how across glass types — which is real and not in the patent. **Not proprietary:** the picosecond laser source, beam-shaping optics, handling, and HF chemistry are almost certainly third-party; LPKF's defensible content is the *combination and recipe*, not the components. ⚠️ **Supplier identities were not verified** and this is the single largest hole in the moat assessment (Caveat 4).

**Verdict: emerging (optionality), not durable.** The IP has been legally tested and held, which is more than most claimed moats can say. But a moat that has **never been priced in a commercial contract** — zero series orders after ~80% design-in — is unproven where it matters.

## 5. Competitive comparison & moat

**Level 2 — the landscape by role.**

- **(a) Direct rivals — TGV/glass-structuring equipment.** **Philoptics** (KOSDAQ) — supplies laser TGV equipment to chipmaker trial lines, plus ABF drilling, direct-imaging litho, singulation, and a TGV hole-inspection kit: five glass-substrate tool categories `[T]`. **Han's Laser** (SZ 002008) — FLEE-TGV femtosecond panel system, claims own IP; the China-localization vector. **Coherent** — ultrafast systems; simultaneously a rival and a probable laser-source supplier. **Disco, Via Mechanics, Trumpf** — named in TGV supplier lists, but ⚠️ no shipped glass-core production tool verified.
- **(b) Adjacent/complementary.** Glass: Corning (3-yr MOU with BOE), Schott, AGC, NEG. Metallization/RDL: Lam Research (RDL on glass panels to 600mm). Inspection: Onto Innovation — and Philoptics' own kit. Korean line suppliers to Samsung EM: Chemtronics, Joongwoo M-Tech.

- **(d) Customers who could vertically integrate.** Samsung EM (**GLASEM JV**, ~KRW 480bn — precisely the structure through which process tooling gets internalized), Absolics/SKC, Intel (pivoting to license/buy), Ibiden (~¥500bn capex 2026–28), Unimicron, AT&S, TSMC.

**Market size, and why the sources disagree by ~20×.** Best-sourced: Counterpoint puts FO-PLP **plus** glass substrate at **$650m (2024) → >$8.1bn by 2030**, AI/HPC 45.6% `[T]` — but that bundles panel-level packaging with glass and is **not** a TGV-equipment TAM. Syndicated reports put "TGV substrate" at $227m–$478m by 2030 with CAGRs spanning **14.3%–34.2%** `[T3]` — a spread so wide it is not decision-useful and should not be cited as fact. ⚠️ **No credible independent TGV-equipment TAM exists** (Caveat 6). The scope confusion is itself the finding: "glass substrate," "TGV substrate," "TGV equipment" and "panel-level packaging" are used interchangeably and differ by more than an order of magnitude.

**Head-to-head: LPKF vs. Philoptics.** Philoptics is the correct comparator — the only named rival confirmed to have *shipped* laser TGV equipment into a chipmaker's glass line, competing at the same Korean accounts.

*Where LPKF wins:* process differentiation (the modify-then-batch-etch mechanism genuinely avoids the microcracking that ablation carries); tested IP; breadth of design-in.
*Where Philoptics wins:* **account proximity and portfolio.** LPKF sells one process step; Philoptics sells five into the same fab. When a customer is de-risking an unproven line, the vendor who can co-own adjacent steps has the structural advantage on line integration. Add Korean domicile — service response, local engineering, currency, industrial-policy preference — next to the two most advanced programs (Samsung EM Sejong, Absolics Covington).
*Shared customers:* **Samsung EM is confirmed dual-sourced**, with both LPKF and Philoptics named as suppliers to the glass line alongside Chemtronics and Joongwoo M-Tech `[T]`.
*Disputes:* none found. The anonymous EPO opposition is the only adversarial IP event on record.
*Who is gaining:* **Philoptics, on trajectory** — not because its process is better (evidence suggests LIDE is technically superior) but because it is expanding tool count per customer while LPKF has spent four-plus quarters converting zero pilot placements into series orders. **Technical lead without order conversion decays.**

**Source-reliability tiering.** Tier 1: LPKF Q1 2026 report, LPKF releases, granted patent documents. Tier 2: TheElec (best single source on this supply chain), TrendForce, Counterpoint, Seoul Economic Daily, peer-reviewed TGV literature, Onto Innovation. Tier 3 — **not presented as fact anywhere in this report**: ad-hoc-news.de (which demonstrably misattributed Q1 financials as Q2 — see Caveat 8), syndicated market-report vendors, Substack "investment maps," X/Twitter.

**Moat verdict: moderate width, emerging-but-fragile durability.** Not wide, for five reasons: the technical approach is converging industry-wide and the literature is publishing the process window; the moat has never been tested under commercial contracting; customer power is extreme (a handful of buyers, all consortium-building, all dual-sourcing); LPKF is sub-scale against Coherent/Lam/Disco-class latent entrants and cannot win a capex or service-footprint war; and management concedes it does not control the gating factor.

**The single most likely way the moat erodes:** a Korean or Chinese equipment maker — Philoptics most likely, Han's Laser as the China variant — ships a modify-plus-etch tool that is *good enough*, designs around the LPKF claim set in jurisdictions where coverage is thinnest, and wins the first HVM line on local service, multi-tool integration, and price. **Because the first series order defines the reference line, and reference lines get copied, losing the first HVM socket is close to losing the decade.** LPKF's own timeline (ramp 2027, HVM 2029) hands a rival a two-to-three-year window to close a gap that is already described in public literature.

LPKF is a **second-derivative** play on AI compute. Notably, `adv-packaging` sector news cuts *opposite* directions for the two names in this watch-list sharing the theme: an early glass-core HVM commitment is confirmatory for LPKF and substitution-risk for IBIDY.

## 8. Risks

**Concentration.** LPKF has **no disclosed material customer** — not one LIDE customer has ever been publicly named by LPKF or confirmed by the customer. Every name in circulation (Intel, Samsung EM, Absolics, LG Innotek, Corning) reaches the market via trade press or inference. **This is a different risk from ordinary concentration: it is *existence* risk.** You cannot size exposure to a counterparty you cannot name.

**Inverted counterparty risk.** With €5.8m of cash and a lender-consent-dependent facility, **LPKF may be the weaker counterparty in its own customer relationships.** A fab qualifying a sole-source tool from a vendor in that position has a supplier-risk problem of its own — which can itself *delay* the orders LPKF needs. This is a reflexive loop and it is not priced by a simple "will the order land" framing.

**Financing / dilution.** The thin float (89.4%) plus a >10% holder whose only exit is the open market, plus a live burn, is the setup for a dilutive raise at a de-rated price. Note AOC is simultaneously running a campaign at Gerresheimer — a stretched activist can be a forced seller.

**Demand-side.** Intel defunded internal glass-core work (Aug 2025). TSMC's rollout is now framed as unlikely before 2030 `[T]`. The Samsung EM GLASEM target is reported as either 2H27 or 2030 depending on source (Caveat 9). Every one of these moved *away* from LPKF's payoff window in the last twelve months.

**Competitive.** Philoptics gaining on account breadth; Han's Laser on Chinese localization; latent entry by far larger toolmakers on a credible HVM signal.

**Upstream.** Corning/Schott/AGC control >90% of low-CTE glass with **12–18 month furnace lead times** — so even a demand inflection cannot convert to LPKF revenue faster than glass capacity allows. ⚠️ No specific committed glass-core capacity number could be verified from any of them (Caveat 6).

**⚠️ The full risk-factor section of the Annual Report 2025 was not read** (Caveat 3). The above is assembled from disclosures and diligence, not from the statutory risk section — a real gap for a report at this depth.

### The Pre-Mortem — the single most likely path to permanent capital impairment

**Not** a technology failure. The named mechanism is this: **glass-core industrialization slips to the 2029–2030 horizon that TSMC and LPKF's own AGM guidance now both imply; LPKF's base business, with backlog already halved to €27m, cannot fund four more years of LIDE development; the company raises equity at a materially de-rated price into an 89.4% float; and by the time the first HVM socket is awarded, the reference line goes to Philoptics on service, integration and price.** LPKF survives as a smaller company holding a validated patent for a market someone else industrialized. The equity does not recover, because the option that justified the multiple was exercised by a competitor.

The tell that this path is live is not the missed 30 June date. It is that **management's own horizon (2029) is longer than the market's (2027), and the cash position is sized for the market's.**

## 10. Sector bottleneck / structural analysis

The binding constraint in glass-core packaging is **not via formation.** It is **downstream qualification** — metallization/RDL adhesion, thermal cycling reliability, warpage at panel scale, and the inspection/metrology needed to prove yield. LPKF has said so itself, in guidance language: ASP volume orders are excluded from FY26 because timing is gated by qualification of downstream steps `[P]`.

This is the structural fact that most changes how the name should be underwritten. **LPKF solved its own step early and is now waiting on everyone else's.** Being first and best at a non-binding constraint does not generate revenue on your schedule — it generates a validated tool sitting in a pilot line while the customer works on a different problem. It also explains the otherwise-puzzling combination of ~80% design-in and zero series orders without needing any story about LPKF's execution.

## 12. Asymmetry verdict

**A genuine option, but a wasting one — and it is closer to a leveraged directional bet than the "low-floor/high-ceiling" framing suggests.**

The ceiling is real and large: a pure-play with tested IP in a market that could grow ~10× has scarcity value with no listed substitute. The floor is *not* zero — €115m of revenue, 67.8% equity, a defensible patent, and plausible strategic value.

## 15. Investment thesis (synthesis)

- **A tested, category-defining patent in the leading approach to a genuinely necessary problem.** Glass cores are the credible answer to ABF's physical limits at AI panel sizes, and LIDE is the process the industry is converging on. **Counterweight:** the convergence is on the *approach*, not on LPKF — the open literature is publishing the process window, and patents protect claims, not a physics category.
- **The only listed pure-play, with ~80% design-in breadth.** If glass industrializes, there is no substitute vehicle, and LPKF is on the learning curve nearly everywhere. **Counterweight:** design-in is not revenue share; 80% design-in has produced **zero** series orders, and the metric is self-reported and unverifiable.
- **The de-rating has already happened.** −50% in nineteen days, sell-side at or below spot, and the funded end of the chain (SKC/Absolics, $783–880m raised) is healthier than a year ago. **Counterweight:** the de-rating repriced the multiple, not the date — and the date moved *away* (TSMC "unlikely before 2030"; management's own HVM 2029).
- **Activist risk resolved into cooperation**, with a board seat rather than a proxy fight. **Counterweight:** it converted escalation risk into overhang risk, and the dilution pressure it partly represented has been replaced by a harder one — a €5.8m cash position against a ~€7m quarterly burn.

**What has to be true:** glass-core packaging industrializes on roughly a 2027–2029 schedule *and* LPKF converts a first series order before the balance sheet forces a raise *and* it holds the first HVM reference socket against a rival gaining on account breadth. Conjunctive, and the middle clause is on the shortest clock.

## 18. Final thoughts & conclusion

LPKF is a technically credible company that solved its part of a hard problem early and is now waiting — with a shrinking backlog and a thin cash position — for an industry to finish solving the rest. The July de-rating was not the market rejecting the technology. It was the market discovering the **duration** of the option, and beginning to ask who funds the wait.

Every thread in this report converges on the same place. The **business model** is a single process step sold into someone else's capex decision. The **backlog** has nearly halved. The **moat** is legally tested but commercially unpriced, and the closest rival is gaining on account breadth rather than technology. The **bottleneck** is downstream and outside LPKF's control, by management's own admission. The **financials** show €5.8m of cash against a ~€7m quarterly burn behind a facility resizable only with unnamed lenders' consent. The **valuation** still embeds ~59–63% of market cap as pure option value on a payoff management dates to 2029. And **sentiment** offers no upgrade channel, with every target at or below spot.

### Pre-investment checklist

- **Your edge — what do you believe that the crowd doesn't?** The crowd — and the prior underwriting of this name — is debating *when the first order lands*, treating 23 July as the referendum. The variant view is that **timing is the wrong axis**: management has already told the market the ramp is 2027 and high volume is 2029, and FY26 guidance excludes ASP volume orders because the gate is downstream qualification LPKF does not control. Reframed, this is not an order-timing trade but a **financing-duration** problem — whether €5.8m of cash plus a €15.5m lender-consent facility bridges to a 2029 payoff without a raise at a de-rated price into an 89.4% float. If you do not hold a specific view on **how the wait gets funded**, you do not have an edge here; you are underwriting a date the company has already walked back.
- **Your tripwire — what would prove you wrong?** Pre-commit to re-underwriting or exiting if ANY of these fire: **(1)** a **second** missed or slipped LIDE first-series-order date after the 30 Jun 2026 miss — credibility gone, the option premium has no basis left to hold; **(2)** an **equity raise executed at a de-rated price**, or a disclosed adverse change to the syndicated facility (reduction, non-extension, or a covenant breach) — the financing leg is the binding constraint; **(3)** a rival TGV method (Philoptics, Han's Laser) wins a **named first HVM socket** — the reference line is the decade; **(4)** the glass volume-production timeline slips materially past 2029 — note this is the horizon management now guides to, not the 2027 the prior tripwire used.
- **Time horizon fit.** The thesis needs **3–5 years** to resolve on management's own numbers. The *tripwires* resolve much faster — within one to four quarters. That mismatch is the whole risk: a multi-year thesis held on a balance sheet financed for quarters.
- **Conviction-appropriate sizing.** **Binary and single-outcome-dependent.** No disclosed customer, one process step, one adoption curve, and a financing event likely required before payoff. This warrants materially smaller exposure than a diversified compounder, and the position should be sized to survive a dilutive raise rather than to be right about the date.
- **What kills the thesis fastest.** A dilutive equity raise at a de-rated price — faster-acting than any technology or competitive outcome, and the specific mechanism in the §8 Pre-Mortem. It is also the tripwire most likely to fire first, because the burn is arithmetic rather than a judgment call.
- **Where the price already sits relative to the thesis.** After −50% in nineteen days, **still pricing the optimistic case on timing** — ~59–63% of market cap is LIDE option value against zero LIDE series revenue and a 2029 HVM date. Cheaper than it was; not yet pricing management's own horizon.
- **What to monitor next.** **23 Jul 2026 H1 report** (series orders; cash after €5.8m; North Star detail) · FY26 order-intake trend · any **named** HVM commitment from Samsung EM/GLASEM, Absolics or TSMC that specifies equipment · **any financing action or facility disclosure** · Philoptics design-win announcements at Samsung EM or Absolics.

**Bottom line.** A real technology, a tested patent, and no listed substitute — attached to a company that must finance a four-year wait on a three-month cash balance. The July crash fixed the multiple, not the calendar. Until the financing question has an answer, the order date is a distraction.
